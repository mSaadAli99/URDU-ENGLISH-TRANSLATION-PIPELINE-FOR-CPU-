Introduction and Beginning
Zainab Faisal: Assalam-o-Alaikum Dr. Majida, welcome to the show. Thank you very much for coming today and taking the time out. Could you give us a small introduction about yourself so our audience listening to you can know what an outstanding academician you are?

Dr. Majida Kazmi: Thank you Zainab, for inviting me to your show. My name is Majida Kazmi and I am a PhD doctor, not an MBBS doctor. It's important to clarify this because once my son said "my mom is a doctor," and people said let's set up a medical camp here, so he had to explain that she's an academic doctor. I did my PhD in Digital Systems Design and I am an Associate Professor in the Computer Engineering Department at NED University. In addition, I am the founding director of a STEM center at NED, which is the first of its kind. I am also a Co-Principal Investigator at the National Center of Artificial Intelligence and head the Embedded Systems and Computer Vision Lab.

Work-Life Balance
Zainab Faisal: You're juggling so many responsibilities at once — when does your family get to see you?

Dr. Majida Kazmi: Yes, most of my time is spent at the university or workplace, but now my family has gotten used to it. I'm able to do all of this because I have a strong support system. They understand my work and know that this is my passion and my oxygen.

Educational Choices and Intent
Zainab Faisal: What led you to enter the STEM field?

Dr. Majida Kazmi: I come from a middle-class family where education was the top priority. At that time, parents only saw two options: engineer or doctor. My mother wanted me to become a doctor, but the hospital environment made me sad, so I chose engineering instead. At the time I didn't know how I'd end up working in healthcare through engineering, but Artificial Intelligence gave me the opportunity to work in industrial automation and the health sector. For me, success means how big the social impact of my work is. This "Power of Intent" is what gets a person up every morning.

Career Challenges and Family Support
Zainab Faisal: Engineering was a male-dominated field — how did you navigate that?

Dr. Majida Kazmi: I did Electrical Engineering, which shaped my critical thinking. I got married during my bachelor's itself. At first I thought it would just become a degree tag, but my husband and in-laws allowed me to continue my career. I started my Master's after my son was born, and then did my PhD. I felt that as a woman and mother, academia was safer for me because fieldwork involves irregular hours.

Self-Guilt and Societal Attitudes
Dr. Majida Kazmi: Working women often suffer from self-guilt, feeling they aren't giving enough time to their children. Society also often makes comments that add to this mental pressure. But my husband shared childcare responsibilities equally, which is why I was able to continue working. There's also a perception at workplaces that women can't handle high-risk tasks, but I believe your emotional intelligence (EQ) is your strength. When you succeed, people say maybe you got a "favor" — you have to ignore all of that and focus on your work.

NED STEM Center and Teacher Training
Dr. Majida Kazmi: When I joined NED in 2018, we realized the new generation wasn't prioritizing engineering. We thought we needed to intervene at the school and college level. We worked on a "Train the Trainer" model, training teachers — especially in government schools — to encourage girls toward STEM. STEM requires a creative mind, and women can bring an excellent perspective to it.

Mentorship and Gen Z
Dr. Majida Kazmi: Teachers and mentors have played a big role in my life, including my supervisor, Dean Dr. Saad, and former Vice Chancellor Dr. Sarosh Lodhi. Now I learn from my own students. There's a misconception about Gen Z that they're lazy, but in my experience they are very talented and full of energy. Their approach is very experimental. I think of my team as a family, where integrity and trust matter most.

Lab Experiences and Encouraging Students
Dr. Majida Kazmi: I don't have any strict criteria for working in my lab — if you're hardworking, the doors are open. Once, a student came from interior Sindh with no recommendation at all; we gave him a chance, and today he has a successful career at a very good firm. This is all "karma" — what you do for others comes back to you from Allah.

Engineering Excellence Awards
Dr. Majida Kazmi: I received awards for two consecutive years. The first project was building AI software for early detection of Diabetic Retinopathy in diabetic patients, based on an idea by Dr. Rehman Siddiqui. The second award was for Urdu digitization. Urdu is among the world's top 10 languages, but its digital footprint is small. We worked on Urdu OCR and text-to-speech to make Urdu more computer-accessible. This involved the blood and sweat of 100 to 150 interns and PhD scholars of mine.

Future Plans: Digital Sustainability
Dr. Majida Kazmi: My next focus is on Digital Sustainability and AI scalability. According to the founder of OpenAI, even saying "thank you" to AI uses a lot of electricity. We need to find eco-friendly and affordable digital solutions. My message to young women is: dream big, and don't see marriage as an obstacle — make it your strength instead. Balance means setting priorities.

Artificial Intelligence and Failure
Dr. Majida Kazmi: AI is just a tool; it cannot replace humans. Whatever creates something cannot become more powerful than its creator. We need to focus on the ethical use of AI and policies around it. As for failure, I see it simply as an experience. When something doesn't work out, I thank Allah that He has planned something better for me. A person gives up mentally before they fail physically — if you're mentally strong, you can do anything.

Conclusion
